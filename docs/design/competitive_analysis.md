## 1. Fi Money

**What it does:** Fi is a neobank for Indian salaried professionals, built around a Federal Bank savings account. It provides automated expense categorisation from SMS/bank feed, savings goals, and an AI assistant ("Ask Fi") for finance queries.

**What it does well:**
- Deep bank integration: auto-reads transactions from the linked Fi account with no manual entry
- Ask Fi AI assistant answers natural language questions about spending ("How much did I spend on food last month?")
- Clean, modern UI targeting the same 22–35 urban professional demographic; strong onboarding

**What it misses or does poorly:**
- Requires opening a Fi savings account — users with existing HDFC/ICICI/SBI accounts face friction or limited integration
- Tracks only transactions through the Fi account; UPI apps (Paytm, PhonePe, GPay) on other bank accounts require manual SMS parsing and are unreliable
- No net worth view aggregating investments, credit card balances, and savings in one number; focus is on spending/saving, not total wealth
- No voice input for logging expenses

**Pricing:** Free (revenue via interchange fees and financial product cross-sells)

## 2. Walnut

**What it does:** Walnut is an Indian expense tracker that reads SMS messages to auto-detect transactions across banks, credit cards, and UPI apps. Acquired by SBI Cards in 2018.

**What it does well:**
- SMS-based auto-detection works across virtually all Indian banks and UPI apps without requiring account linking
- Broad payment coverage: handles credit cards, debit cards, net banking, and UPI in one place
- Expense analytics and category-level monthly summaries are straightforward and useful

**What it misses or does poorly:**
- Development has been slow/stagnant post-SBI acquisition; UI is dated and has not kept pace with competitors
- Requires SMS read permission, which users are increasingly reluctant to grant (privacy concern); Android 12+ restrictions have degraded reliability
- No investment tracking, no net worth view; purely an expense logger
- No AI categorisation beyond rule-based keyword matching; miscategorisation is common
- No voice input

**Pricing:** Free

## 3. YNAB — You Need A Budget

**What it does:** YNAB is a budget-first personal finance app built around the "zero-based budgeting" method — every rupee/dollar is assigned a job before it is spent. Popular globally among users who want strict financial discipline.

**What it does well:**
- The zero-based budgeting methodology is the most effective system for users who actively want to change spending behaviour
- Strong educational content (video courses, live workshops) that builds financial literacy alongside the tool
- Multi-device sync, bank import for supported banks (US-centric), and a well-designed web + mobile experience

**What it misses or does poorly:**
- Designed entirely around the US/Western financial system; no Indian bank integrations, no UPI, no INR-specific features
- Steep learning curve — the methodology requires commitment; casual users churn quickly
- Expensive at ~$14.99/month or ~$99/year; unjustifiable for Indian users who have free alternatives
- No net worth dashboard; investment tracking is minimal; the product is purely about budgeting, not wealth

**Pricing:** Paid — ~$14.99/month or ~$99/year; 34-day free trial

## 4. Mint (Shut Down Jan 2024)

**What it does:** Mint was Intuit's free, US-centric personal finance dashboard aggregating bank accounts, credit cards, investments, and loans into a single view. Intuit shut it down in January 2024, migrating users to Credit Karma.

**What it does well (historical):**
- Pioneered the all-accounts-in-one-place dashboard concept; net worth view was central, not an afterthought
- Auto-categorisation across linked accounts with decent accuracy for US merchants
- Free tier was genuinely full-featured, which drove mass adoption

**What it misses or did poorly:**
- US-only bank integrations (Plaid); no support for Indian banks, UPI, or INR
- Categorisation accuracy degraded over time; the product received minimal investment in its final years
- No voice input, no AI assistant, no mobile-first design
- Shut down entirely — no longer an active competitor, but its failure validates the market gap for a Mint-equivalent built for India

**Pricing:** Was free; now defunct

## 5. Splitwise

**What it does:** Splitwise is a shared expense tracker focused on splitting bills among groups — flatmates, travel groups, couples. It tracks who owes whom and simplifies debt settlement.

**What it does well:**
- The best-in-class UX for group expense splitting; debt simplification algorithm is genuinely useful
- Works well for Indian use cases (flatmates, trips); UPI deep-links for settlement are supported in the Indian version
- No bank account linking required — lightweight and privacy-friendly

**What it misses or does poorly:**
- Not a personal finance tracker; cannot replace a ledger for individual daily expenses, investments, or net worth
- No income tracking, no budget features, no savings goals
- Free tier limits history; Splitwise Pro (~$3.99/month) is required for full export and receipt scanning

**Pricing:** Freemium — free for core splitting; Splitwise Pro at ~$3.99/month

## 6. Groww

**What it does:** Groww is an Indian investment platform for mutual funds, stocks, ETFs, and digital gold. It provides a portfolio dashboard showing holdings, returns, and asset allocation.

**What it does well:**
- Clean investment tracking: P&L, XIRR, and portfolio allocation visualised well
- Covers mutual funds (direct plans), equity, IPOs, and FD — most assets an Indian retail investor holds
- Zero commission on mutual funds; strong trust in the Indian market with a large user base

**What it misses or does poorly:**
- Investment-only — no expense tracking, no credit card management, no daily transaction ledger
- Does not aggregate accounts held outside Groww (e.g., existing HDFC Securities demat, PPF, EPF); net worth view is incomplete
- No voice input, no AI categorisation, no WhatsApp integration

**Pricing:** Free for mutual funds; standard brokerage for equity trades

## 7. Money Manager

**What it does:** Money Manager is a manual double-entry ledger app (Korean origin, popular across India and Southeast Asia). Users enter every income and expense manually; it produces reports and balance sheets.

**What it does well:**
- Highly detailed: supports accounts, categories, subcategories, payees, and notes — more granular than most apps
- One-time purchase model; no subscription; works offline entirely
- Popular with power users who want full control over their financial record-keeping

**What it misses or does poorly:**
- Entirely manual — no auto-import, no SMS reading, no bank sync; friction is high for daily use
- UI is dated and dense; the learning curve for double-entry accounting is a barrier for non-accountants
- No AI features, no voice input, no sharing or collaboration
- No net worth dashboard that aggregates investments alongside expenses

**Pricing:** Freemium — free tier with limited features; premium at ~$3.99–$7.99 one-time purchase (varies by platform)

## 8. Spendee

**What it does:** Spendee is a European personal finance app with a strong visual design, supporting manual entry and bank connections (Europe and US). It tracks budgets, shared wallets, and provides spending analytics.

**What it does well:**
- Best-in-class visual design among expense trackers; charts and category breakdowns are genuinely readable
- Shared wallets feature is useful for couples or flatmates tracking joint expenses
- Supports manual entry and bank sync (for supported regions); cross-platform (iOS, Android, web)

**What it misses or does poorly:**
- No Indian bank integrations; UPI, Paytm, PhonePe are not supported
- Expensive for the feature set: ~$2.99/month or ~$23.99/year for bank connections and unlimited wallets
- No voice input, no AI categorisation beyond basic tagging
- No investment tracking or net worth view; purely expense/budget focused

**Pricing:** Freemium — free for manual entry (limited wallets); premium ~$2.99/month or ~$23.99/year

## Where to Differentiate

### 1. Voice-first input — the only app that lets you speak your expenses

No competitor in this list supports voice input for logging transactions. The interaction friction of opening an app, tapping through categories, and typing an amount is the primary reason expense trackers fail — users stop logging after 2–3 weeks.

Net Worth Calculator's core interaction is spoken: "Aaj 340 rupaye medicines ke liye diye Paytm se" → transaction logged, confirmed, done. This is a genuine capability gap in the market, not a marginal feature difference.

The WhatsApp delivery channel amplifies this: users do not need to install or open a dedicated app. The interaction happens in the messaging app they already have open 50+ times a day.

### 2. India-specific by design, not by adaptation

Fi Money and Walnut are India-native but neither handles the full picture of how urban Indians actually transact:

- Multiple UPI apps (Paytm, PhonePe, GPay, CRED Pay) mapped to multiple bank accounts
- Credit cards from Axis, ICICI, HDFC, Scapia, SBI — each with different billing cycles and reward structures
- Mixed expense types: Kharche (daily), CC bills (periodic), transfers (Adhoc), investments (irregular)
- English, Hindi, and Hinglish in the same sentence

The schema and voice parser are built around these realities from day one, not retrofitted.

### 3. Net worth as the primary output, not an afterthought

Mint proved the market wants a single net worth number. Groww tracks investments but not expenses. Walnut tracks expenses but not investments. Fi does neither well. No Indian app currently shows:

```
Assets: savings (₹X) + investments (₹Y) + UPI wallet balance (₹Z)
Liabilities: credit card outstanding (₹A) + pending transfers (₹B)
Net Worth: ₹(X + Y + Z - A - B)
```

This is the core output of Net Worth Calculator. The transaction log is the input; net worth is the answer.

### 4. Zero cost, permanently

YNAB at $99/year and Spendee at $24/year create churn risk for price-sensitive users. Fi and Walnut are free but monetise through financial product cross-sells, which creates misaligned incentives (push credit cards, not financial health).

Net Worth Calculator is free because it is a personal tool, not a SaaS business. No upsells, no premium tier, no data monetisation. This is a trust advantage with the target demographic.

### 5. AI categorisation that learns the user's patterns

Walnut uses keyword matching. Fi uses rule-based categorisation from merchant codes. Neither learns from corrections or handles ambiguous Hinglish descriptions.

Net Worth Calculator's AI pipeline (LLM-based extraction from voice/text) handles:
- Merchant name inference from partial descriptions
- Account mapping from payment app (Paytm → SBI, GPay → HDFC)
- Category inference from context ("medicines", "petrol", "EMI")
- Correction feedback loop via WhatsApp reply

## Key Risks from Competitor Analysis

1. **Fi Money's AI roadmap** — Ask Fi is improving rapidly. If Fi extends to cross-bank aggregation and adds voice input, it could close the gap. Monitor quarterly.
2. **Walnut's SMS advantage** — Despite stagnation, Walnut's SMS-read approach has wide coverage. Android permission restrictions are tightening and will erode this over time — neutral for Net Worth Calculator.
3. **No moat on voice alone** — Voice input is buildable by any competitor with LLM access. The moat is India-specific context (AppMapping, payment ecosystem, INR handling) + WhatsApp delivery channel + the existing data set once the user has 6 months of history.
4. **Groww expanding** — If Groww adds expense tracking and net worth aggregation, it has the user base to become dominant. Low probability near-term given their investment-platform focus.
